12 Things First-Time SA Home Buyers Miss — And What They Cost to Fix

First-time home buyers in South Africa don't lose money because they're careless. They lose money because nobody told them what to look for. These 12 issues are the most expensive surprises — and every one of them is visible during a thorough viewing, if you know where to look.

1. Fresh Paint Covering Water Damage

A freshly painted ceiling is a red flag, not a feature. Look for discolouration around ceiling joins, soft spots, or a faint musty smell in upper rooms. Active leaks get painted over, not fixed. Roof repairs, ceiling replacement, and mould remediation can cost R80,000+.

2. Geyser Age

The installation date is printed on the unit. A geyser over 10–12 years old is approaching end of life. Budget R8,000–R15,000 for replacement, and note that a burst geyser can cause R50,000+ in secondary damage if the drip tray isn't correctly installed.

3. Structural vs Cosmetic Cracks

Diagonal cracks running from the corners of windows or door frames indicate foundation movement — not settlement. Straight, hairline cracks are usually cosmetic. The difference between the two is potentially R50,000–R200,000 in underpinning or foundation work.

4. Body Corporate Debt (Sectional Title)

Before buying into a complex, request the latest body corporate financials and AGM minutes. A complex with R2 million in outstanding maintenance debt will recover it through special levies — charged to all owners, including you, the day you take transfer.

5. Illegal Extensions and Alterations

A converted garage, enclosed patio, or added bedroom without municipal approval has no compliance certificate. Your bank may refuse to bond the property. Your insurer may void your policy. Always ask for building plans approved by the municipality.

6. Drainage Around Foundations

Walk the garden perimeter. Soil should slope away from the house. Pooling water against a foundation causes long-term structural damage. Lush green patches in dry weather, watermarks on external walls, or efflorescence (white salt deposits) are warning signs.

7. Electrical Panel Age

Old ceramic fuse boxes are a fire risk and fail modern insurance requirements. Upgrading to a compliant DB board with RCD protection costs R8,000–R15,000. Full rewiring of an older home starts at R25,000.

8. Roof Condition

Step outside and look up. Cracked or missing tiles, sagging gutters, rust on flashings, and moss growth are all visible warning signs. A full roof replacement starts at R40,000. Don't assume a selling agent would have flagged this — they often don't.

9. True Monthly Ownership Costs

Your bond repayment is one number. Add municipal rates, levies (for complexes), home insurance, water, electricity, and a realistic monthly maintenance provision. Many first-time buyers find their total monthly housing cost is 40–60% higher than the bond payment alone.

10. Water Pressure

Run taps in every bathroom simultaneously. Low pressure can indicate corroded galvanised pipes that need full replacement — a job that costs R20,000–R40,000 in an older home.

11. Neighbourhood Trajectory

A house is also an investment. Check crime statistics, school ratings, and any approved development plans nearby. A beautiful home in a declining area may be worth significantly less in five years.

12. No Pre-Approval Before Viewing

Viewing properties without a pre-approval certificate means you don't know your real budget. Most SA banks can pre-approve you in 24 hours. Without it, you risk falling in love with a property you can't finance — or losing it to a buyer who came prepared.

The pattern: every one of these issues is visible during a thorough property viewing. None of them require a professional inspector to flag initially — they require knowing what to look for and actually looking.
