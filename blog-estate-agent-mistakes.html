10 Property Defects South African Estate Agents Overlook (And Why It Costs Buyers Thousands)

Good estate agents know property. They know neighbourhoods, market values, and how to negotiate. What they're not trained to do is spot a 15-year-old geyser on the verge of failure, or a boundary wall built without an engineer's sign-off. These are the 10 defects that most commonly slip through during SA property showings.

1. Body Corporate Financial Health

For sectional title properties, agents rarely request the body corporate's financials. A complex that owes R2.5 million in outstanding maintenance levies will pass that burden directly to new owners through special levies. Always ask for the latest AGM minutes and levy statement before making an offer.

2. Unpermitted Alterations

A converted garage, enclosed patio, or added bedroom without municipal approval means no compliance certificate — and no bond approval from most banks. Agents often present these additions as selling features without disclosing the legal risk they carry.

3. Geyser Age and Condition

The average geyser lasts 8–12 years. A 15-year-old geyser isn't a feature — it's a liability. Replacement costs R8,000–R15,000. Ask to see the installation date on the unit itself, and check whether it has a drip tray and overflow pipe correctly installed.

4. Fresh Paint Hiding Water Damage

A freshly painted ceiling can look pristine and conceal months of active water ingress. Look for discolouration around ceiling joins, slight bubbling in paint, or musty smell in upper rooms. These are signs of roof leaks painted over rather than fixed.

5. Foundation and Structural Cracks

Diagonal cracks running from door or window corners are a structural red flag, not a cosmetic issue. They indicate foundation movement. Agents rarely distinguish between these and hairline settlement cracks. The difference can be R50,000–R200,000 in remediation costs.

6. Electrical Panel Age

Old ceramic fuse boxes are both a fire risk and an insurer's red flag. Upgrading to a modern DB board with RCDs costs R8,000–R15,000. Many SA homes over 20 years old still have non-compliant electrical panels that agents don't flag.

7. Drainage Around the Property

Poor drainage causes foundation damage that costs tens of thousands to fix. Look for soil sloping toward the house, watermarks on the foundation walls, or lush green patches in dry weather — all signs that water is pooling where it shouldn't.

8. Boundary Walls Without Compliance

In South Africa, boundary walls over a certain height require engineering sign-off. Walls built without it are uninsurable and the owner's liability. Collapse of a non-compliant wall — as seen in multiple SA insurance cases — results in a rejected claim.

9. Roof Age and Condition

Most buyers never look up. Most agents don't either. Inspect the roof from outside — cracked tiles, missing flashing, sagging guttering, and rusted fasteners are all visible. A full roof replacement in SA starts at R40,000 for a standard home.

10. True Monthly Ownership Costs

Agents quote the purchase price and sometimes the bond repayment. They rarely add rates, levies, insurance, water, electricity, and a maintenance provision. Total monthly costs can be 40–60% higher than the bond payment alone — a shock many buyers only discover after moving in.

This isn't criticism of estate agents. They're trained to sell, not inspect. That's why buyers — and agents who want to avoid deal-collapsing surprises late in the process — need their own structured assessment tool at every viewing.
